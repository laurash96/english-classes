Teaching a true beginner — mindset

The first class must end with a win — no exceptions. A true beginner carries decades of "I can't learn languages" as a self-belief. Your job in Class 1 isn't to teach grammar — it's to break that belief. They need to leave saying: "I said things in English. I understood things. I can do this."

Silence is not failure — it's processing. At Pre-A1 level, silence after a question can mean "I'm translating", "I'm afraid", or "I'm trying". Wait at least 8–10 seconds before stepping in. If they still can't answer, model the answer yourself and move on — never make silence awkward.

Spanish is your best ally, not your enemy. Use it to explain meaning, confirm understanding, and reduce anxiety. The goal is never to avoid Spanish — it's to use English as much as possible while ensuring nothing is confusing.

The Pre-A1 golden rule: Every class should have at least one moment where the student says something in English and you genuinely react as if it's meaningful — because it is. "I'm María" from someone who has never spoken English before is a breakthrough. Treat it like one.
⚠️ What to avoid: Metalanguage (don't say "third person singular"). Overwhelming vocabulary lists. Comparing them to faster learners. Finishing sentences for them too quickly. Asking "Does that make sense?" — they'll say yes even when it doesn't. Instead ask them to try it: "Can you say it yourself?"

At Pre-A1 level, keep the tech as simple as possible. Every new tool is cognitive load that takes away from language acquisition. The fewer apps they need to manage, the better.

The Hook — Warm-up10 min

Activity: "You already know English"

Most true beginners don't realize how much English they already know passively — from movies, music, social media, technology. This activity proves it and immediately lowers the affective filter.

1
Ask: "¿Qué palabras en inglés ya conoces? Las que sean." Let them list freely in the chat or verbally. Common answers: OK, coffee, hello, bye, yes, no, sorry, please, Instagram, selfie, email, Google, Netflix, app, zoom, password.
2
Celebrate each word genuinely. Count them together. "Tienes 15 palabras en inglés y ni siquiera lo sabías." Point to the screen: "These are English words. You said them. In English."
3
Now model: "My name is [your name]. I'm from [city]. I'm a teacher." Say it slowly, type it on screen. Then: "Now you — just your name and where you're from." They say it. You react as if it's the best thing you've ever heard — because for them, it is.
Why this works: Evidence beats encouragement. You're not telling them they can do it — you're showing them they already have been doing it. That's a completely different conversation.
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge22 min

Challenge: "Hello, I'm…"

Build their first complete English self-introduction — sentence by sentence, together. By the end, they can say 5 things about themselves in English with no help.

1
Build together (10 min): Start with the sentence on screen: "My name is ___." They fill in the blank aloud. You add the next: "I'm from ___." Then: "I'm ___ years old." Then: "I'm a ___." (job). Then: "I'm [adjective] today." Each time — they say it, you write it on screen.
2
Read it back (5 min): All 5 sentences are on screen. They read them aloud — their own words, about themselves. This is the moment. Don't rush it. Let them feel what it's like to speak English.
3
Hide the screen (5 min): Turn off screen share. Ask them to say as many of the 5 sentences as they can remember, in any order. What sticks is acquisition. What doesn't — comes back next class.

Error Spotting — Gentle version8 min

At Pre-A1, error spotting is NOT about fixing mistakes — it's about hearing the difference. Show two options on screen and ask: "Which sounds right to you?" No pressure, no grades.

I am have 30 years.
✓ I'm 30. / I'm 30 years old.
In English, age uses "be" — not "have". Say: "En inglés la edad se dice con 'am/is/are', no con 'have'."
My name is María, I am teacher.
✓ My name is María. I'm a teacher.
Jobs need "a" before them in English. Just model it — don't explain "articles" yet.
At Pre-A1, the correction is always positive: "Almost! Here's the English version:" — then model the correct sentence and ask them to repeat it once. That's enough. Move on.

The Hook — Warm-up8 min

Activity: "What do you see?"

1
Ask them to look around and describe 5 objects they see — in English if possible, Spanish if not. For each Spanish word, you give them the English equivalent and type it on screen. They say it back. "Tengo un vaso → I have a glass."
2
Review their WhatsApp voice note from last class. Read back one sentence they said correctly: "Last week you said 'I'm a teacher' — perfect. Today we add more."
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge24 min

Challenge: "My Profile"

Fill in a simple profile card — in English — about themselves. Then describe a person they know using "She/He has" and "She/He is". The profile is in the shared Google Doc.

1
My section (8 min): Open the shared doc. Fill in: name, age, city, one thing I have (pet, car, house…), one thing I like. Do it together — you type, they dictate in any language, you translate and model the English version.
2
My person (10 min): Pick one person important to them. Describe them using: "Her name is…", "She is ___ years old.", "She has ___.", "She is ___." Sentence frames on screen. They dictate, you model and write.
3
Read aloud (5 min): They read both sections aloud. You listen for fluency — not perfection. React naturally to what they share.

Session 03 · 60 minutes
Every Day
Present Simple · Daily verbs · What you do, every single day
Present Simple
🔥
The Hook — Warm-up8 min

Activity: "Emoji Day"

1
Ask them to send 5 emojis in the Zoom chat that represent their typical day — in order. No words yet. ☕ 💻 🍕 📺 😴
2
You point to each emoji and together build the sentence: "☕ → Every morning I drink coffee." Type it on screen as they say it. You're co-authoring their day in English.
5 emojis = 5 English sentences. They've already produced their first Present Simple paragraph without realizing it.
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge24 min

Challenge: "My Day" — shared doc

Write a short description of their typical day in the shared Google Doc. They use sentence frames. You help translate any Spanish words they get stuck on. Then they read it aloud.

1
Write (12 min): Open the class 3 template. Fill in their daily routine — morning, afternoon, night. Minimum 6 sentences. Spanish words in parentheses are fine. You watch and help in real time via the shared doc.
2
Read aloud (6 min): They read the whole thing out loud. Don't interrupt for errors — let them finish. Then highlight 2 sentences they said perfectly.
3
Quick swap (5 min): You describe your day with the same structure. They listen and try to understand. Ask one Yes/No question to check comprehension: "Do I drink coffee?" "Yes!"

Error Spotting8 min

Use sentences from their own doc (anonymized or with permission). Show them on screen.

Every day I no drink coffee.
✓ Every day I don't drink coffee.
"No" doesn't negate a verb in English. "Don't" does. This is the most important thing to fix at Pre-A1.
I working at home.
✓ I work at home.
Present Simple uses the base verb (no -ing). The -ing form (Present Continuous) comes much later.

Session 04 · 60 minutes
Yes or No?
Do you…? / Are you…? · Short answers · The joy of asking and answering
Questions
🔥
The Hook — Warm-up8 min

Activity: "True or False?"

1
You say 3 things about yourself — 2 true, 1 false. Slowly and simply. They listen and guess which is false. They answer: "Number 2 is false!"
2
Now they do the same — 3 sentences about themselves. You guess. The goal: they produce 3 English sentences from memory, under zero pressure, because the game makes it fun.
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge24 min

Challenge: "Ask me anything"

They ask you 5 questions in English using the question frames on screen. You answer truthfully. Then you ask them 5 questions — they answer with short answers.

1
Their turn (10 min): Question frames are on screen. They pick 5 and ask you. You answer in full sentences so they hear natural English. They can look at the screen throughout — reading a question aloud counts as speaking.
2
Your turn (10 min): You ask them the same questions. They answer with short answers. Prompt: "Just say 'Yes, I do' or 'No, I don't' — that's a complete sentence in English."
3
Celebration (4 min): Count together how many English sentences they've produced across these 4 classes. Give a genuine number — it will be more than they expect. Celebrate it.

¿Eres / Estás ___?
Do you like ___?
¿Te gusta ___?
Do you have ___?
¿Tienes ___?
Do you work / live / eat ___?
¿Trabajas / vives / comes ___?

Error Spotting8 min
You like coffee?
✓ Do you like coffee?
Questions in English need "Do/Does" — you can't just raise intonation like in Spanish. Show the difference by saying both versions aloud.
Yes, I like.
✓ Yes, I do! / Yes, I like coffee!
Short answers can't end on the main verb alone. "Yes, I do" is a complete, natural short answer.
