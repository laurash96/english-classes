Pedagogical approach

A1-A2 is not "easier" — it's different. The grammar is simpler but the emotional vulnerability is higher. Beginners need to feel safe to attempt anything. Every correct sentence is a win worth celebrating.

More scaffolding, same methodology: Dogme ELT and TBL still apply — students produce language first, structures emerge second. The difference is you provide more sentence frames, more visual support, and more patience with silence.

L1 support is a feature, not a failure: At A1-A2, brief Spanish explanations accelerate understanding. Use it strategically — explain meaning in Spanish, but always model the English version immediately after.

Teacher mindset shift for A1-A2: Celebrate every attempt. A student who says "She is… my mother… very funny" has just produced three meaningful English words. That is acquisition. Don't rush to correct form — reflect it correctly and move on.
🖥
Online setup — A1-A2 specific

At A1-A2 level, instructions in English must be simple and always paired with a demonstration. Never just say — always show. Screen sharing is your most powerful tool.

For the teacher
🖥 Google Slides (visual prompts)
🗒 Jamboard (word mats, images)
📄 Shared Google Doc (templates)
⏱ Timer visible on screen
For the students
📱 WhatsApp group
🎙 Mic on, camera on
📝 Shared doc (pre-opened)
Key A1-A2 rule: Always have a visual on screen. An image, a word mat, or sentence frames reduce anxiety dramatically. Students who can see the scaffolding take more risks with the language.
Session 01 · 60 minutes
Nice to Meet You
Verb To Be (am / is / are) · Basic identity · First introductions
Verb To Be
🔥
The Hook — Warm-up 8 min

Activity: "5 Words About Me"

1
Go first. Type 5 words in the Zoom chat that describe you — any words, any language — and read them aloud: "Smart. Tired. Coffee. Dogs. Impatient." Then model how to turn them into English sentences: "I'm smart — well, I try. I'm always tired. I love coffee."
2
Each student types 5 words in the chat — Spanish or English, no pressure. Then reads them aloud. You help turn each word into a simple "I am / I'm" or "I love / I hate" sentence. Write their sentences on Jamboard as they speak.
3
Count the sentences on the board. Point out: "Look — you already said 8 things about yourself in English. That's the whole point of today."
Online tools
💬 Zoom chat for word dump 🗒 Jamboard to build sentences live
Why it works: Starting with single words (not full sentences) removes the pressure of grammar. The structure emerges naturally as you model it. They produce their first English sentences without realizing they're "doing grammar."
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge 25 min

Challenge: "Language Exchange — First Day"

They're at a virtual language exchange event. They have to introduce themselves to different people — 3 rounds, 3 different "meeting" scenarios. Each round gets slightly more complex.

1
Round 1 — Basics (5 min): Screen shows the prompt: Name? City? Job? Each student introduces herself to the other with just these 3 things. Sentence frame on screen: "Hi! I'm [name]. I'm from [city]. I'm a [job]." They can read from screen — fluency over memory at this stage.
2
Round 2 — Add feelings (8 min): New prompt added: How are you today? One thing you love? One thing you hate? Frame: "I'm [feeling] today. I love [noun]. I hate [noun]." You play the "other person" and model natural reactions: "Oh really? Me too!"
3
Round 3 — No frames (7 min): Remove the sentence frames from screen. They introduce themselves one more time, freely. What they remember, they keep. What they forget, they improvise. You note what sticks naturally vs. what drops — that's your roadmap for correction.
Online tools
🖥 Google Slides with prompts per round ⏱ Visible timer per round
Target structures: "I'm [name]", "I'm from…", "I'm a…", "I'm [adjective]", "I love / I hate…"

Debrief line: "Look — in Round 3 you didn't need the screen. That's because those words are already yours."
✨

🔍
Error Spotting — Common A1 Errors 8 min

Dynamic: "Fix It Fast" — Show 4 short sentences on screen. One is correct, three have errors. They tap / type the error in the chat. Keep it fast and game-like.

I am have 28 years.
✓ I'm 28. / I'm 28 years old.
Age uses Verb To Be, not "have". Classic Spanish interference — "tengo 28 años" → "I have 28 years" ✗
My name is María, I am student.
✓ My name is María. I'm a student.
Missing article "a" before a singular job/role. In Spanish there's no article, in English there is.
I no like mornings.
✓ I don't like mornings.
Negation in English uses "don't" — "no" alone doesn't work as a verb negator.

Session 02 · 60 minutes
My People
He / She / They + Have/Has · Describing family and close friends
He / She / Have
🔥
The Hook — Warm-up 8 min

Activity: "Show Me Your Phone"

1
Ask each student to open their phone gallery and find one photo with a person they love. They share their screen or hold the phone up to the camera. Don't give any language instructions yet — just ask them to show and describe it naturally in whatever mix of English and Spanish comes out.
2
Repeat what they said, but in correct English, slightly expanded: If she says "ella es mi hermana, es muy… funny", you say: "She's your sister. She's funny. That's perfect — you just described her in English." Write it on the Jamboard.
3
The other student asks ONE question about the person in the photo. Sentence frame on screen: "Is she / he…? How old is she / he? Does she / he live near you?" One question only — celebrate every attempt.
Why personal photos work: The emotional investment is immediate. Students want to talk about the people they love — and that motivation overrides the fear of making mistakes. He/She/His/Her emerge naturally from the description.
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge 26 min

Challenge: "The Missing Person"

Each student describes one important person in her life — in English. The other student listens, asks questions, and draws or writes down what she imagines that person looks like and what they're like. No photos allowed during the description.

1
Preparation (3 min): Each student picks one person (family member, best friend, anyone important). They have the shared Google Doc open with the template: Name, age, job, personality, one habit. Give 3 minutes to write notes in Spanish or English — whatever comes first.
2
Description (8 min each): Student A describes her person aloud. Student B listens and draws / writes key words in her doc section. Then B can ask up to 3 questions: "Is she tall?", "How old is she?", "Does she have [siblings]?" Then swap.
3
Reveal (5 min): Each student shows the photo of her person. How close was the other's mental image? You highlight language on screen — particularly He/She is… and He/She has…
Online tools
📄 Shared Google Doc (template) 🖥 Screen with scaffolding phrases
Scaffolding on screen during the task:
"She / He is…" · "She / He has…" · "She / He loves / hates…" · "She / He works as a…" · "She / He lives in…"

Key grammar contrast to highlight: "She is funny" (personality) vs. "She has brown hair" (physical). This is the first time they encounter this distinction — don't over-explain, just point it out.
✨

Error Spotting 8 min

Dynamic: "Is or Has?" — Show 6 sentences on screen. Students type "IS" or "HAS" in the chat before the sentence appears complete. Fast, game-like, low stakes.

She have brown eyes.
✓ She has brown eyes.
Third person singular: "have" → "has". One of the most common A1 errors — needs repetition in context, not explanation.
My sister is a brown hair.
✓ My sister has brown hair.
Physical features use "have/has" in English — not "be". Direct interference from "mi hermana es morena."
He is very funny person.
✓ He is a very funny person.
Missing article "a" — same pattern as Class 1. Worth reinforcing at A1-A2 level.

Session 03 · 60 minutes
A Typical Day
Present Simple · Daily actions · What you do, love, and avoid
Present Simple
🔥
The Hook — Warm-up 8 min

Activity: "Emoji Timeline"

1
Each student types 5 emojis into the chat that represent her typical day, in order. No words — just emojis. Example: ☕😴💻😩🛋️
2
The other student tries to guess what each emoji means for that person: "☕ = you drink coffee in the morning?" The owner confirms or corrects — in English: "Yes! I always drink coffee first." / "No — that's my cat's food, actually."
3
You collect the verbs that came up naturally (drink, wake up, check, eat, watch) and write them on the Jamboard. These become the vocabulary of the task.
Why emojis work at A1-A2: They give beginners something concrete to talk about without requiring them to produce language from nothing. The image does the scaffolding work — they just need to put words to it.
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge 26 min

Challenge: "Pen Pal Letter"

They're writing to a new English-speaking pen pal who wants to know about their life. They write a short letter in the shared Google Doc — what they do every day, what they like, what they don't like. Then they read it aloud and the teacher plays the pen pal who replies.

1
Write (10 min): Using the template in the shared Google Doc, each writes her letter. Minimum 6 sentences. Sentence frames are on screen as support. Allow Spanish words — circle them and fix together after.
2
Read aloud (5 min each): Each reads her letter out loud. The other listens and reacts naturally: "Really? Me too!" / "Oh, interesting!" You note 2–3 errors silently.
3
The pen pal replies (8 min): You play the pen pal and respond in slow, clear English — asking 2 questions. Students answer in real time. This is their first real listening + speaking interaction in English.
Sentence frames on screen during writing
Every day, I wake up at…
Todos los días me levanto a las…
I usually [verb] in the morning.
Normalmente [verbo] por la mañana.
I love [noun/verb-ing].
Me encanta [sustantivo/verbo].
I don't like [noun/verb-ing].
No me gusta [sustantivo/verbo].
At night, I [verb].
Por la noche, yo [verbo].
✨

Error Spotting 8 min

Use real sentences from today's letters (anonymized). Show them on screen. Students identify the error and fix it — this is the most powerful error work because they recognize their own language.

Every day I no drink coffee.
✓ Every day I don't drink coffee.
Negation = "don't / doesn't" — "no" as a standalone negator doesn't work in English sentences.
She wakes up and drink coffee.
✓ She wakes up and drinks coffee.
Third person -s must appear on every verb in the sentence, not just the first one.
I like very much the music.
✓ I really like music. / I love music.
Word order: "very much" goes at the end in English, or use "really" before the verb. No article before uncountable nouns like "music."

Session 04 · 60 minutes
Do You…?
Yes/No questions · Short answers · The art of asking and reacting
Questions
🔥
The Hook — Warm-up 8 min

Activity: "True or False?"

1
Each student types 3 sentences about herself in the chat — 2 true, 1 false. Simple sentences using what they know: "I have a dog. I love horror movies. I wake up at 5am."
2
The other student asks one Yes/No question to investigate: "Do you really have a dog?" The owner answers with a short answer: "Yes, I do!" / "No, I don't." You model the short answer format if needed.
3
After 3 rounds, debrief: "Did you notice? You just asked and answered questions in English. That's what today is about."
Key insight for A1-A2: Short answers ("Yes, I do" / "No, I don't") feel unnatural but are essential for fluency. The game format makes them repeat the structure many times without it feeling like drilling.
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge 26 min

Challenge: "The Magazine Interview"

Each student plays a celebrity (of her choice) being interviewed for a magazine. The other plays the journalist. Then they swap. They must use questions and short answers throughout.

1
Prep (3 min): Each student picks a celebrity or fictional character — or even a version of themselves 10 years in the future. She writes 3 facts about this person in the shared doc. The journalist (the other student) prepares 5 questions using the question frames on screen.
2
Interview Round 1 (10 min): Journalist asks, celebrity answers. At least 5 questions. Short answers required: "Yes, I do / No, I don't / Yes, I am / No, I'm not." Question frames stay on screen throughout.
3
Swap + debrief (10 min): Roles reverse. You then pick 3 questions from the interview and ask them yourself — to both students — to consolidate the question-answer structure.
Question frames on screen (journalist toolkit)
Are you…?
¿Eres / Estás…?
Do you like…?
¿Te gusta…?
Do you have…?
¿Tienes…?
Do you work / live / eat…?
¿Trabajas / vives / comes…?
Is it true that…?
¿Es verdad que…?
✨
Vocabulary Boost — A2 8 min
yes, I do!
¡sí! (short answer) — not just "yes"
no, I don't
no (short answer) — always with "don't"
not really
no mucho / no exactamente — soft no
it depends
depende — "Do you like mornings? It depends."
no way!
¡para nada! / ¡qué va! — strong reaction
are you serious?
¿en serio? — express surprise
what about you?
¿y tú? — the most important question to model
🔍
Error Spotting 8 min

Dynamic: "Question Doctor" — Show broken questions on screen. Students fix them before they can ask you the question. If they fix it correctly, they get to ask you (you answer honestly). Turns the correction into a game.

You like dogs?
✓ Do you like dogs?
Yes/No questions in Present Simple need "Do/Does" at the start — can't just raise intonation like in Spanish.
Yes, I like.
✓ Yes, I do! / Yes, I like dogs!
Short answers in English can't end on the main verb. Use "I do" or repeat the full noun.
Does she likes coffee?
✓ Does she like coffee?
"Does" already carries the 3rd person — the main verb stays in base form (no -s).
