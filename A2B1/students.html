Who Are You at Parties?
Class 1 · Verb To Be · Who you are beyond your job title
🎉

Today you'll talk about yourself in English — the real version, not the professional one. Your friendships, your family, your weird habits. Grammar shows up on its own when you have something real to say.

📋
Before class — checklist
Camera working and ready (it's non-negotiable ✓)
Think about: "I'm the friend who always…" — what's your answer?
Think of one thing people always get wrong about you
Bring the energy — mistakes are part of the plan
⚡
Your mission today ~33 min

A Friend of a Friend's Birthday: You've been invited to a dinner where you only know the host. Everyone else is new, some are international. Make conversation for 45 minutes over dinner. First with a character, then as yourself.

1
You get a character card in the private chat. You are her for 10 minutes. Introduce yourself, find connections, keep conversation going — even if the pasta is overcooked.
2
Mid-dinner surprise: the teacher (as another guest) asks you both: "So, what's your story? How do you know each other?" Tell it in English — make it good.
3
Round 2: drop the character. Same party, same table — but it's actually you. What changes? What's easier to say when you're being real?
Phrases that will come out naturally: "I'm the one who… actually I'm not sure how to explain it." "She's actually the reason I…" "We're both from… it's complicated, but in a good way."

Common errors — know them before they happen
✗ Common
I am have 32 years.
✓ Natural
I'm 32.

In English, age uses "be", not "have". Simple but easy to forget when thinking in Spanish.

✗ Common
She is engineer in Google.
✓ Natural
She's an engineer at Google.

Two things: "an" before engineer, and at for companies, in for cities/departments.

Sundays & Other Lies
Class 2 · Present Simple · Real routines, family habits and what you actually do
☀️

Today you'll describe your real life — Sundays, family dynamics, and habits. The "-s" in third person will show up on its own when you're talking about someone you actually know.

📋
Before class
Think of 5 verbs from your typical Sunday (just verbs — wake, call, eat, avoid, regret…)
Send your voice note + reply from WhatsApp Mission #1
Open the shared Google Doc before joining the call
⚡
Your mission today ~38 min

Welcome to My Life: A foreign friend is visiting you for the first time. She doesn't know your family, your city, or your weekend rituals. Write her a guide — in English.

1
Each of you writes your section in the Google Doc: one covers family life (who's who, who calls every Sunday, what your mom thinks you should do differently), the other covers weekend life (what you eat, what you watch, what you avoid). 10 minutes of real writing.
2
Read your section aloud. The other plays the curious foreign friend: "Does your mom actually call every day?", "What does your sister do?", "How often do you see them?"
3
The teacher color-codes the text: green = affirmative, orange = negative, blue = question. Present Simple mapped from your own words.
Phrases that will appear naturally: "My mom calls every Sunday — I don't always pick up." "She lives close by. She works in education." "Does your family eat together?" — "Not really — we tend to do our own thing."

Common errors
✗
She work from home.
✓
She works from home.

With he/she/it → always add "-s" to the verb. No exceptions.

✗
Does she works late?
✓
Does she work late?

The "-s" lives in "does" — the main verb stays in base form. Only one "-s" per sentence.

Spill the Tea ☕
Class 3 · Synthesis · Describing people, retelling situations, and gossip as grammar
👀

Today you describe people, recount situations, and give opinions — all in English. Gossip is the best grammar workout. You'll be using Verb To Be and Present Simple without even noticing.

📋
Before class — homework
Write 3–4 sentences describing someone in your life (friend, family, coworker) — anonymously. Send to the group before class.
Don't say their name or relationship — just describe them and let the class guess.
Review sentence starters from the previous class
Description example: "She's the one who always has drama but somehow it's never her fault. She works in something creative — I think marketing? She's actually really funny once you get past the initial chaos."
⚡
Your mission today ~38 min

The Blind Date Report: Student A just came back from a blind date set up by Student B. B needs a full debrief — in English. What was he/she like? Worth a second date?

1
Student A gets a character card (name, job, personality, one quirk) in private chat. She's the one who went on the date. Student B knows nothing — she just set it up.
2
A debriefs to B — everything. B asks questions: "What does he do?", "Is he funny?", "Is he the kind of person who…?" 15 minutes of pure gossip in English.
3
B gives her verdict — second date or not? Then they swap: B went on the date, A asks the questions. The teacher throws in one surprise question.
Grammar naturally produced: "He's… / He works as… / He doesn't seem like…" "Does he live alone?" / "Is he the type to…?"

Caught in the Act
Class 4 · Present Continuous · Describing what's happening right now
🎬

Today you'll narrate muted videos like radio commentators. The most fun class so far — and your brain won't even notice it's learning grammar.

📋
Before class
Send the DM draft from Mission #3 and leave feedback for your classmate
Have something to be doing when you turn on your camera (drinking coffee, wearing headphones, looking at the ceiling — whatever)
Think about this (no Google): what's the difference between "I work from home" and "I'm working from home"?
⚡
Your mission today ~36 min

Silent Movie Commentary: The teacher shares a muted video on screen. You are the commentators. Narrate everything in English, in real time.

1
Clip 1: both of you talk at the same time, like radio hosts. No turns. The chaos is intentional — just keep going.
2
Clip 2: swap every 15 seconds. One describes the action, the other adds detail or context. The teacher times the switch.
3
Debrief: the teacher shows two columns with your own phrases — Simple (habit) vs Continuous (now). The difference reveals itself.
Phrases that will come out naturally: "She is opening her laptop — she looks exhausted." "He is checking his phone again. He always does that." "They seem to be arguing about something."
✨

Simple vs Continuous — the key difference
Present Simple
She works from home.
Present Continuous
She's working from home today.

Simple = habit, routine, general truth. Continuous = happening now or during this period.

✗ Error
I am knowing the answer.
✓ Correct
I know the answer.

State verbs (know, like, want, understand, believe) don't go in Continuous. They're states, not actions.
