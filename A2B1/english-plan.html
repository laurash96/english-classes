Pedagogical approach

Dogme ELT: Grammar emerges from real conversation, not the other way around. Students produce language from minute one — the teacher never introduces a structure cold.

Task-Based Learning: Each class revolves around a real-world task (meeting strangers at a party, recounting your friend's drama to someone who wasn't there, narrating a video in real time). Grammar is the vehicle, not the destination.

Low affective filter: Adult learners acquire language faster when they feel safe and respected. The vibe is colleagues talking, not student and teacher. Errors are data points, not failures.

Teacher reminder: You are a facilitator, not a transmitter. Aim to talk less than 30% of the time. Let communicative chaos happen — that's exactly where real acquisition occurs.
🖥

The 2-student advantage: With only two learners, each one speaks twice as much as in a larger group. There's nowhere to hide — and that's exactly what accelerates acquisition. The teacher acts as a third participant whenever the dynamic calls for it and can give highly personalized, real-time feedback. Breakout rooms are not needed — all activities happen in the main session.
Session 01 · 60 minutes
Who Are You at Parties?
Verb To Be · Personal identity · Who you are beyond your job title
Verb To Be
🔥
The Hook — Warm-up 8 min

Activity: "The Friend Who Always…"

1
Share the Jamboard with 5 sentence starters. Each student completes them in writing — 3 minutes, no pressure: "I'm the friend who always…" / "I'm the one who never…" / "I'm the daughter who…" / "My friends say I'm…" / "People are always surprised that I'm…" — If they get stuck on a word, write it in Spanish and keep going.
2
Go first with your own answers to model the tone: "I'm the friend who always has a plan B — and a plan C. I'm the one who never arrives on time but always texts ahead. My friends say I'm a lot, but in a good way." Make it funny, human, and a little embarrassing. The more real, the better.
3
Each student reads her answers aloud. The other reacts in English: agree, disagree, recognize herself in it ("That's so you!" / "Wait, she's literally the same!" / "I would never say that about myself but it's true.") — Capture every Verb To Be instance in yellow on the Jamboard. Count them at the end.
Online tools
🗒 Jamboard with 5 sentence starters pre-loaded 💬 Zoom chat for real-time reactions 🎥 Camera ON — facial reactions are half the fun
Why it works: Talking about who you are as a person (not as a professional) is lower stakes and more emotionally resonant. Verb To Be floods the conversation naturally: "I'm the friend who…", "She's the one who…", "We're both…". No grammar lesson needed.
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge 25 min

Challenge: "A Friend of a Friend's Birthday"

A mutual friend is throwing a birthday dinner. You've been invited but you only know the host — everyone else is new. Some guests are international. You need to make conversation for 45 minutes over dinner.

1
Round 1 — first impressions (10 min): You send each student a private role card in the chat (e.g. "You're the host's cousin from Medellín who now lives in Berlin. You're a bit introverted but very funny once people get to know you."). They have to introduce themselves and keep conversation going. You play one of the other guests at the table.
2
Mid-dinner moment (8 min): Introduce a social awkwardness prompt via chat: "Someone at the table just asked you both: 'So, what's your story? How do you two know each other?'" — They have to tell the story of their friendship in English, spontaneously. Pure Verb To Be + personal vocabulary.
3
Round 2 — as themselves (8 min): Drop the characters. Same party, same table, but it's actually them. What changes? What's easier to say when you're being real? Debrief: show 3 phrases from Round 1 vs. 3 from Round 2 — which felt more natural?
Online tools
📄 Role cards sent via private Zoom chat 🗒 Jamboard for live debrief ⏱ Visible timer on screen
Target phrases to emerge: "I'm the one who…", "She's actually the reason I…", "We're both from…", "Are you the one who…?", "It's complicated — but in a good way."

Teacher launch script: "Ok. You're both at a birthday dinner. You know the host. Everyone else is new, some people don't speak Spanish. It's already awkward. You have to make conversation. The pasta is overcooked. Go." — Then stay in character as another guest.

Error Spotting — Fossilization 10 min

Dynamic: "Sentence Hospital" — You share your screen with the sick sentences in Google Slides. They are the doctors. Alternating turns: one diagnoses, the other prescribes the fix. You only speak if neither of them gets to the answer.

Online tools
🖥 Shared Google Slides ✏️ Zoom Annotate to mark the errors 💬 Chat to type the correction before saying it
I am have 32 years.
✓ I'm 32 years old. / I'm 32.
Spanish interference: "tener" años → in English, age uses Verb To Be, not "have".
She is engineer in Google.
✓ She's an engineer at Google.
Missing article "an" + wrong preposition: at for companies, in for cities/departments.
We are very excited for this project.
✓ We're very excited about this project.
Collocation: excited about (not "for"). Very common with Spanish speakers.

Session 02 · 60 minutes
Sundays & Other Lies
Present Simple · Real routines · Family, weekends and the things you actually do
Present Simple
🔥
The Hook — Warm-up 7 min

Activity: "Whose Sunday is This?"

1
Go first: describe your real Sunday in 4–5 sentences, no filter: "I wake up late. I call my mom — she always calls first anyway. I pretend I'm going to do laundry, I don't. I watch three episodes of something and feel vaguely guilty. I start Sunday-panicking about work at 7pm." Model the casual, honest tone.
2
Set a 90-second timer. Each student writes their Sunday in verbs only into the chat — just base forms, as many as they can (wake, call, eat, scroll, ignore, avoid, regret, repeat…). No full sentences yet. Speed over perfection.
3
Copy verbs to the Jamboard in two columns: "I" and "She". Build sentences aloud alternating person: "I call my mom, she calls her mom…" Wait for them to notice the "-s" without prompting. If they don't in 2 minutes: "Do you notice anything different between the two columns?"
Online tools
💬 Zoom chat for verb dump (90s timer) 🗒 Jamboard live — two columns (I / She)
Hidden objective: They notice the third-person "-s" inductively, before anyone mentions the word "rule". Family and weekend routines produce more emotionally real sentences than work routines.
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge 28 min

Challenge: "Welcome to My Life"

A foreign friend is visiting you for the first time. She doesn't know anything about your life — your family, your city, your weekend rituals. Each student writes a short "welcome guide" to their real life in a shared Google Doc.

1
Writing (10 min): In the shared Google Doc, each writes their section: one covers family life (who lives with you, who calls you every Sunday, what your mom thinks you should do differently), the other covers weekend life (what you do, what you avoid, what you eat, what you watch). Timer visible. Authentic > perfect.
2
Oral read + cross-examination (10 min): Each reads her section aloud. The other plays the visiting friend who's curious and asks follow-up questions: "Does your mom actually call every day?", "What does your sister do?", "How often do you see them?" You join as a third curious voice when things get interesting.
3
Color-coded reflection (8 min): Screen-share the Google Doc and highlight: green = affirmative, orange = negative, blue = question. The full Present Simple map emerges from their own authentic words — not a textbook example in sight.
Online tools
📄 Live collaborative Google Doc 🖥 Screen share with color highlighting ⏱ Timer visible on screen
Why family/weekend works better than work: Students have stronger opinions and emotions about personal routines — they argue, laugh, correct each other. That emotional charge drives better acquisition than describing a professional workflow.

Debrief line: "Look at your own words on the screen. You just built the Present Simple — affirmative, negative, questions — from scratch. Want me to show you the 'official' version now?"

Error Spotting — Fossilization 10 min

Dynamic: "The Grammar Lie Detector" — You say 5 sentences about your real life, 2 of which are false. They vote in Mentimeter or the Zoom chat on which ones are lies — but they also hunt for any grammar error. Double game.

5 example sentences for this dynamic (adjust to your real life):
1. "She works from home every day." → ERROR: "work" missing -s
2. "I don't understand nothing about quantum computing." → ERROR: double negative
3. "Does she works late on Fridays?" → ERROR: "works" with Does
4. "I speak three languages." → correct
5. "My cat sits on my laptop every time I have a meeting." → correct (or is it a lie?)
They vote FIRST on what's a lie, THEN spot the grammar error. Two levels of attention at once.

Online tools
📊 Mentimeter for real-time voting 💬 Zoom chat as quick alternative 🖥 Pantalla compartida con las frases
She work from home every Friday.
✓ She works from home every Friday.
The most deeply fossilized error: dropping the "-s" in 3rd person singular. Work on this specifically through oral repetition in context, not mechanical drilling.
I don't understand nothing about this model.
✓ I don't understand anything about this model.
Spanish double negative ("no entiendo nada"). In English, "don't" already carries the negation — use "anything", not "nothing".
Does she works late?
✓ Does she work late?
The "-s" belongs in the auxiliary "does" — the main verb stays in base form. Classic error when the rule was memorized instead of acquired.

Session 03 · 60 minutes
Spill the Tea
Synthesis · Describing people · Gossip as the ultimate grammar vehicle
Synthesis
🔥
The Hook — Warm-up 8 min

Activity: "Who Is She?" — Anonymous description game

1
Before class, each student sends you 3–4 sentences describing someone in their life without naming them (a friend, a family member, a coworker). "She's the one who always arrives late but somehow always has an excuse. She works in marketing but she acts like she runs the company. She's actually really funny once you get to know her."
2
You paste the descriptions anonymously on the Jamboard — one per sticky. Students read them aloud and speculate: "This sounds like it could be a sister… or maybe a coworker? She works in marketing so…" The author stays silent. Pure listening + comprehension.
3
The author reveals who it is and how close the guesses were. She adds one detail they didn't include — in English. Notice: this activity is basically Verb To Be + Present Simple + third person -s, all in context, all organic.
Online tools
🗒 Jamboard with anonymous descriptions pre-loaded 💬 Zoom chat for guesses in real time
Why gossip is gold for language learning: It's 100% third person ("she is", "she does", "she always…"), which is where the most common errors live. Students are highly motivated — they want to talk about people they know. The descriptions are rich, nuanced, and emotionally real.
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge 28 min

Challenge: "The Blind Date Report"

Student A just came back from a blind date set up by Student B. B needs a full debrief — in English. What was he/she like? What do they do? Were they worth a second date? This forces both Verb To Be and Present Simple in third person, with opinions, reactions, and follow-up questions.

1
Setup (3 min): Send Student A a character card via private chat: name, job, personality traits, one quirk. She's the one who went on the date. Student B gets nothing — she only knows she set it up. The date is about to be reported.
2
The debrief (15 min): A reports back to B. B asks everything: "What does he do?", "Is he funny?", "Does he live alone?", "How tall is he?", "Is he the kind of person who…?" You observe and note. Only intervene if they get stuck for more than 10 seconds.
3
Verdict + swap (10 min): B gives her verdict — second date or not? Then they swap: now B went on the date (new character card), A asks the questions. You throw in one surprise question from "the date's perspective" to keep them on their toes.
Online tools
💬 Private Zoom chat for character cards 🗒 Jamboard for live quote capture ⏱ Timer visible on screen
Grammar naturally produced: "He's…" / "He works as…" / "He doesn't seem like…" / "Does he live…?" / "He's the kind of person who…" — this is the complete Present Simple paradigm + Verb To Be in a fully authentic context.

Alternative task (equally good): "The Group Chat Drama" — give them a fictional WhatsApp conversation with some drama and ask them to reconstruct what happened, who said what, and what each person is like.
✨

Error Spotting — Fossilization 8 min

Dynamic: "The Tea Got Cold" — You share real sentences from today's debrief conversation (anonymized), some correct, some with errors. They fix the grammar before they can continue the story.

Online tools
🖥 Google Slides with real quotes from class 💬 Zoom chat for corrections
She has 30 years and she work in finance.
✓ She's 30 and she works in finance.
Two fossils in one sentence: "have" for age + missing third-person -s. Both from the same Spanish interference pattern.
He seem like a nice person, to be honest.
✓ He seems like a nice person, to be honest.
Third-person -s on "seem" — easy to drop when the sentence feels natural otherwise.
I'm agree that he's not the right one.
✓ I agree that he's not the right one.
"Agree" is a verb, not an adjective. It never needs "be" — direct calque from Spanish.

Session 04 · 60 minutes
Caught in the Act
Present Continuous · Describing what's happening now · Real-time narration
Present Continuous
🔥
The Hook — Warm-up 8 min

Activity: "What Are You Doing Right Now?" — Camera reveals

1
Everyone joins with the camera off. You give the signal — each person turns on their camera while doing something specific: holding a coffee, staring at the ceiling, wearing headphones, etc. No prior coordination.
2
Alternating turns: describe what the other is doing right now. "You're holding a mug. She's looking at me. The cat is sitting on the desk." 60 uninterrupted seconds each.
3
Capture the structures that emerged in the Jamboard. Ask: "What's the difference between 'She works from home' and 'She's working from home'?" — They answer before you explain anything.
Online tools
🎥 Camera ON with live action 🗒 Jamboard to capture structures live ⏱ 60-second timer per turn
Why it works: Present Continuous describes situations happening right now, right in front of them. There's no more real context than that. The contrast with Present Simple emerges on its own.
⚡
The Task — Main Challenge 26 min

Challenge: "Silent Movie Commentary"

You share a short muted clip on screen (office scene, cooking video, city documentary, a work meeting gif). They narrate what they see in real time, out loud, in English.

1
Clip 1 — parallel (7 min): Both comment at the same time without coordinating, like radio hosts who can't see each other. You play the clip. The communicative chaos is intentional — both practice without waiting for turns.
2
Clip 2 — coordinated duet (8 min): They swap every 15 seconds. One narrates the action ("The woman is opening her laptop"), the other adds detail or context ("She's wearing headphones — she's probably in a meeting"). You time the switch.
3
Debrief: Simple vs Continuous (10 min): Back to the Jamboard, two columns. Using real phrases they produced, build the distinction between habit (Simple) and action in progress (Continuous) from their own language.
Online tools
🖥 Pantalla compartida con el video mudo 🗒 Jamboard para el contraste Simple vs Continuous ⏱ Timer de relevo (15 seg) visible 🎙 Microphone ON at all times
Recommended clips: The Office office scene (muted, S01E01 first 3 min are perfect) · city time-lapse · narration-free cooking video · any work team gif. Avoid videos with visible subtitles.

Managing the "coordinated duet" (Clip 2): Put a 15-second timer on screen. When it rings, the second student takes the mic without the first finishing her sentence. The abrupt handover is intentional — it forces active listening and picking up the thread. If they laugh, even better: cortisol drops and linguistic input goes up.

Opening debrief line: "I wrote down everything you said in the last 20 minutes. Look — you just used the Present Continuous naturally, without thinking about it. Now let's look at the times you used Present Simple for the same people. What's the difference?"

Online tools
🖥 Google Slides with situations 💬 Zoom chat for simultaneous answers
I am knowing the answer.
✓ I know the answer.
State verbs (know, understand, believe, like, want) never go in Continuous. They describe permanent states, not actions in progress.
She is work from home today.
✓ She's working from home today.
Missing gerund (-ing). Continuous always requires be + verb-ing. No exceptions.
They are always losing their keys.
✓ They always lose their keys. (habit)
✓ They're always losing their keys. (irritation)
Special case: "always + Continuous" is valid and expresses irritation or surprise at a repeated habit. Both forms are correct but carry different nuances — worth exploring with them.
